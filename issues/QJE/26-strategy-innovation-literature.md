## Why they touch the paper

- **Henderson & Clark (1990)**: "Architectural innovation" = changes in how existing components are linked together, not the components themselves. Close cousin of the paper's distinction between step-level AI capability (components) and chain-level organization (architecture). Automation that reconfigures architecture rather than components is exactly the model's long-run margin.
- **Baldwin (2007/2008)** on modularity and firm boundaries: formalizes transaction-free zones separated by "thin crossing points" — analogous to the handoff-cost boundaries this paper models at the step/chain level. Potentially maps AI chains ↔ transaction-free zones, and handoffs ↔ thin crossing points.
- **Baldwin (2023)**: the natural overview citation if the paper wants to engage Baldwin's broader modularity agenda succinctly.
- **Novak & Wernerfelt (2012)**: formal model of how interdependence between parts governs make-or-buy. Direct theoretical cousin of the job-design problem (Issue 03 Option 2): how should interdependent tasks be grouped into organizational units?
- **Wernerfelt (1997)**: adjustment costs as a theory of firm boundaries — parallel to this paper's specialization-vs-coordination tradeoff. The abstract-level isomorphism is worth an explicit citation.
