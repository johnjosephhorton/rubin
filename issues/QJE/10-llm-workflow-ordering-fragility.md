# Issue 10 — LLM-imputed workflow ordering is fragile and time-bound

## Raised by
**R2 (§3 "What is the weak link?") + editor.** This is the single most-cited empirical concern.

> Editor: "We (especially R2, R4, and I) have concerns with the plausibility and durability of your core empirical work imputing workflow adjacency of tasks in occupations."
>
> R2: "O*NET task lists do not observe production sequences; they are catalogues of job activities. The paper substitutes an LLM's narrative reconstruction of 'typical' task order for an observed workflow, and all subsequent adjacency-based objects are functions of that imputation."
>
> R2: "The LLM may systematically cluster tasks by semantic similarity (e.g. writing-related tasks adjacent to one another) in ways that are mechanically correlated with AI executability, which can inflate 'chain' patterns."
>
> R2: "The mapping from observed conversations to 'automation' versus 'augmentation' is platform- and period-specific… these empirical relationships should be interpreted as contingent snapshots of a rapidly moving target."

## Verification against source
- Empirical section builds the occupation panel in `empirics.tex` using O*NET tasks + Eloundou exposure + AEI execution + GPT-generated orderings. Confirmed.
- Robustness checks for alternative prompts are in `writeup/appendix-GPTprompts_robustness.tex` and plots `writeup/plots/GPT_task_sequences_overlap_analysis/`.
- No currently-implemented test against a *non-LLM* ground truth for task ordering.

## Options to address

1. **External validation against observed workflows.** Find an independent non-LLM source for task order — for example:
   - Production-process studies in IO/management literature (narrow but ground-truth)
   - Time-use diaries (ATUS) to recover within-day task sequences
   - Log data from project-management tools (proprietary; hard to get)
   Validate GPT orderings against these for a subset of occupations.
2. **Address semantic-clustering concern head-on.** Run a placebo where the "ordering" is generated by semantic similarity only (e.g. BERT embedding distance), and show chaining patterns are stronger with workflow ordering than with semantic ordering.
3. **Durability hedge.** Add a theoretical section clarifying exactly what the empirics can (clustering of *AI-use-events* within constructed sequences) and cannot (stable long-run chain structure) establish. Scope the empirical claims more narrowly.
4. **Embrace option 3 of R4** — reframe as AI adoption, where the AEI/Eloundou gap is the outcome, not the ordering itself. Ordering becomes a covariate whose imputation error washes out under appropriate controls.

## Effort vs. payoff

| Option | Effort | Payoff |
|---|---|---|
| 1. External validation | High (several weeks data work; uncertain availability) | Very high if successful — blunts the sharpest criticism |
| 2. Semantic-similarity placebo | Medium (1–2 weeks; pure compute) | **High** — cheap, directly refutes R2's specific mechanism |
| 3. Scope-of-claim hedge | Low (2–3 days) | Medium — necessary either way |
| 4. Reframe | (See Issue 03) | High — sidesteps the problem |

**Recommendation:** Option 2 is the single highest-ROI empirical response. Pair with Option 3 for framing, and plan Option 1 for the following revision cycle if Option 2 is not sufficient. Option 4 is a structural decision that feeds back to Issue 03.
