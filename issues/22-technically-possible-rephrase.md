# Issue 22 — p.25 "technically possible" hedge for more specialization under AI

## Raised by
**R3 (other comments, second bullet).**

> "On p.25, it's not clear to me why the possibility that AI leads to more specialization is presented as only 'technically possible'. It seems intuitive to me that this would happen sometimes, and as Example V shows, AI could make it optimal to combine more tasks into a single job under conditions that don't seem outlandish. I would cut 'technically' or spend longer explaining why you think this is extremely unlikely in practice."

## Verification against source
- Confirmed at `discussion.tex:131`: "It is also technically possible in our model for AI to increase the amount of specialization in an optimal job design."
- R3's reading is reasonable: the hedging language undersells a result Example V actually supports.

## Options to address

1. **Drop "technically".** Replace with "it is also possible (see Example V) that AI increases specialization, if…". Stop hedging a theoretically-supported outcome.
2. **Elaborate the conditions.** Briefly characterize which parameter regions produce more specialization under AI (e.g. when AI handoff costs are low and AI-performed steps are cheap relative to human supervision).
3. **Empirical cross-reference.** If any existing result from the empirical section speaks to specialization changes (even anecdotally), cite it here as suggestive evidence.

## Effort vs. payoff

| Option | Effort | Payoff |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Drop "technically" | Trivial (2 min) | Medium — direct response to reviewer |
| 2. Elaborate conditions | Low (½ day) | High — turns a throwaway line into a testable prediction |
| 3. Empirical cross-ref | Low (½ day if data exists) | High — ties theory back to data |

**Recommendation:** Option 2 minimally; Option 3 if feasible. This is cheap shelter against R3 and incidentally strengthens the paper's positioning as a job-design theory (Issue 03).
