# Comparison of Related Papers on AI, Tasks, and Labor

Comparison of the Rubin task-chaining paper with four closely related papers, in the style of Table 1 of Budish (2011, JPE) on alternative mechanisms for combinatorial assignment.

**Papers compared:**

1. **Rubin (Task Chaining)** — this paper
2. **Garicano, Li, Wu (2026)** — *"Weak Bundle, Strong Bundle: How AI Redraws Job Boundaries"*
3. **Fukui, Nakamura, Steinsson (2026)** — *"The Commoditization of Labor"*
4. **Gans, Goldfarb (2026)** — *"O-Ring Automation"* (NBER WP 34639)
5. **Hampole, Papanikolaou, Schmidt, Seegmiller (2025)** — *"Artificial Intelligence and the Labor Market"* (NBER WP 33509)

## How the Rubin paper sits in this group

A one-line positioning per pairwise comparison:

- **vs. Garicano–Li–Wu.** Both endogenize where the "job" ends. They do it on a 2-task Cobb–Douglas with a scalar coordination cost; we do it on an *ordered $m$-step* Leontief with chain-level verification, so *where in the sequence* AI is strong matters, not just *how strong* it is.

- **vs. Fukui–Nakamura–Steinsson.** Their margin is labor-market power (commoditization), not task assignment — they keep workers in every task and change *who captures the surplus*. Ours is the opposite: it keeps surplus-sharing competitive and changes *which steps the worker performs*.

- **vs. Gans–Goldfarb.** Both reject task-by-task substitution under complementarity, but the mechanisms differ. Their non-separability is *quality-multiplicative within a single worker's time budget* ("focus"); ours is *sequential with verification as a chain-level fixed cost* ("chaining"). They get lumpiness from time reallocation; we get it from collapsing adjacent steps into one verifiable task.

- **vs. Hampole–Papanikolaou–Schmidt–Seegmiller.** Closest neighbor on the empirical side; their *concentration of exposure* is the natural distributional analogue to our *fragmentation*. The key conceptual difference is that their tasks are exchangeable — concentration is just how unequal the within-occupation exposure distribution is — while our fragmentation is *spatial in the workflow*: it cares whether exposed steps sit adjacent to one another or are dispersed. Their concentration predicts more labor demand (workers reallocate); our fragmentation predicts less AI execution (no chains can form). These are orthogonal slicings of within-occupation exposure structure and should be jointly informative.
