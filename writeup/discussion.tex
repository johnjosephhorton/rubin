\section{Discussion}

In this section we draw out three implications of the model.
First, we show that AI chaining can overturn comparative-advantage logic: a step that is cheaper to perform manually in isolation may nonetheless be automated in the optimal configuration of production (Section~\ref{sec:comparative_advantage}).
Second, we introduce a \emph{fragmentation index} that captures how dispersed a job's AI-suitable steps are, and show that it approximates the cost of the firm's optimal AI strategy---so that the gains from AI depend on the arrangement of steps, not merely on how many are AI-suitable (Section~\ref{sec:fragmentation}).
Third, we show that improvements in AI quality can affect production cost non-linearly, with marginal gains that rise once better AI makes longer chains worthwhile (Section~\ref{sec:nonlinear}).

\subsection{Comparative Advantage and AI Chaining}
\label{sec:comparative_advantage}

For a single step in isolation, the decision whether or not to deploy AI depends simply on whether the manual execution cost exceeds the AI management cost---that is, on the step's AI exposure alone.
For two or more steps, however, the optimal deployment of AI depends on more than each step's exposure in isolation, because of the benefits of automating multiple steps together in an AI chain.
As a result, a step that is more cost-effective to perform manually \emph{in isolation} may nonetheless be automated as part of a chain: the factor of production holding the comparative advantage on a step in isolation need not be the one that performs it in the optimal configuration of production.
The following example illustrates this breakdown of comparative-advantage logic.

\begin{example}
Consider a two-step process with unit skill, so a task's cost equals its expected completion time, with the following step parameters:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{cccc}
\toprule
Step ($i$) & \makecell{Manual\\$(\manualSkill{i}, \manualTime{i})$} & \makecell{AI mgmt.\\$(\AIskill{i}, \AItime{i})$} & \makecell{AI success\\prob.\ ($q_i$)} \\
\midrule
1 & $(1, 1)$ & $(1, 2)$ & $0.9$ \\
2 & $(1, 4)$ & $(1, 1)$ & $0.9$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
In isolation, step~1 is cheaper \emph{manually}---its manual cost $\manualSkill{1}\manualTime{1} = (1)(1) = 1$ is below its augmentation cost $\AIskill{1}\AItime{1}/q_1 = (1)(2)/0.9 \approx 2.2$---while step~2 is cheaper with \emph{AI}, since $\AIskill{2}\AItime{2}/q_2 = (1)(1)/0.9 \approx 1.1$ is below its manual cost $\manualSkill{2}\manualTime{2} = (1)(4) = 4$.
\input{plots/TikZ_visualization/comparative_advantage_visualization.tex}
Yet the cost-minimizing strategy chains both steps and automates step~1. The chain's cost,
\[
\frac{\AIskill{2}\,\AItime{2}}{q_1\, q_2} = \frac{(1)(1)}{(0.9)(0.9)} = \frac{1}{0.81} \approx 1.23,
\]
falls below the comparative-advantage assignment---step~1 manual and step~2 augmented---which costs
\[
\manualSkill{1}\manualTime{1} + \frac{\AIskill{2}\,\AItime{2}}{q_2} = (1)(1) + \frac{(1)(1)}{0.9} \approx 1 + 1.11 = 2.11,
\]
and below every other strategy.
Automating step~1 inside the chain removes its verification cost---paid once, at the chain's augmented endpoint---leaving only the joint-success penalty ($q_1 q_2 = 0.81$).
The human's comparative advantage on step~1 is thus overturned: in the optimal configuration the step is automated, at zero direct cost.
\end{example}

More generally, the same reversal can be guaranteed in \emph{any} production process---not only the two-step example---by a sufficient condition relating step $k$ to its AI-executed neighbors.

\begin{proposition}[A sufficient condition for overturning comparative advantage]
\label{prop:ca_threshold}
Consider any production process and a step $k$ on which the human holds the comparative advantage in isolation, $\manualSkill{k}\manualTime{k} < \AIskill{k}\AItime{k}/q_k$. Suppose that in the cost-minimizing strategy steps $k+1, \dotsc, r$ form an AI chain with augmented endpoint $r$, and that the \emph{incremental} cost of drawing step $k$ into that chain is below its manual cost:
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ca_sufficient}
\frac{\AIskill{r}\AItime{r}}{\prod_{i=k+1}^{r} q_i}\left(\frac{1}{q_k}-1\right)
\;=\;
\frac{\AIskill{r}\AItime{r}\,(1-q_k)}{\prod_{i=k}^{r} q_i}
\;<\;
\manualSkill{k}\manualTime{k}.
\end{equation}
Then that strategy does not perform step $k$ manually: folding step $k$ into the chain---automating it---is strictly cheaper, so the human's isolated comparative advantage on step $k$ is not exercised.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Suppose a strategy runs $(k+1, \dotsc, r)$ as an AI chain but performs step $k$ manually. Folding step $k$ into the chain drops the manual task (saving $\manualSkill{k}\manualTime{k}$) and changes the chain's expected cost from $\AIskill{r}\AItime{r}/\prod_{i=k+1}^{r} q_i$ to $\AIskill{r}\AItime{r}/\prod_{i=k}^{r} q_i$, since the AI must now succeed at step $k$ as well. The resulting change in total cost is
\[
\frac{\AIskill{r}\AItime{r}}{\prod_{i=k}^{r} q_i}
- \frac{\AIskill{r}\AItime{r}}{\prod_{i=k+1}^{r} q_i}
- \manualSkill{k}\manualTime{k}
\;=\;
\frac{\AIskill{r}\AItime{r}\,(1-q_k)}{\prod_{i=k}^{r} q_i} - \manualSkill{k}\manualTime{k}
\;<\; 0
\]
by \eqref{eq:ca_sufficient}. Every other task is untouched, so the strategy that performs step $k$ manually is not cost-minimizing.
\end{proof}

The condition is sufficient, not necessary, and the same reversal arises in many other configurations. It is slack for two reasons. First, automating step $k$ is charged only the \emph{incremental} failure risk $(1-q_k)$ rather than the full chain cost: the human checkpoint at the chain's endpoint $r$ is already paid for, so appending step $k$ adds no new verification. Second, the firm needs only \emph{one} favorable downstream endpoint $r$ to satisfy \eqref{eq:ca_sufficient}. The reversal therefore occurs more readily the more AI-suitable step $k$'s neighbors are---a higher joint success $\prod_{i=k+1}^{r} q_i$ along the adjacent chain---and the costlier step $k$ is to perform manually. For the two-step process of the example, \eqref{eq:ca_sufficient} reduces to $q_2 > \AIskill{2}\AItime{2}(1-q_1)/(q_1\,\manualSkill{1}\manualTime{1})$, which there is also necessary; in longer processes step $k$ can be drawn into AI execution through chain arrangements that \eqref{eq:ca_sufficient} does not capture, so the condition only \emph{understates} when the reversal occurs.

Whether step~1 is automated depends on its neighbors.
Had step~1 instead sat beside a step that AI completes only with low probability, the chain's joint success probability would be small, inflating its cost, and step~1 would revert to manual execution.
The same step, with identical characteristics, can therefore be automated in one workflow yet performed manually in another, depending only on the AI-suitability of its neighbors.
This is precisely the variation we exploit in Section~\ref{sec:DWA_prediction}, where we compare the realized execution of conceptually identical steps across occupations whose neighboring steps differ in AI-suitability.



\subsection{Job-level AI Exposure and the Fragmentation Index}
\label{sec:fragmentation}

Returning to the firm's deployment problem described in \eqref{eq:totalcost_shortterm} and Section~\ref{sec:optimization.short}, we can interpret job-level AI exposure as the extent to which an optimal AI strategy employs AI automation and augmentation.
Intuitively, a run of \emph{consecutive} steps in the production process that an AI can perform effectively is a natural candidate for an AI chain.  
A job that contains such runs of consecutive steps is therefore likely to benefit most from AI automation.  
On the other hand, a job for which AI-friendly steps are separated by intermediate steps that an AI is likely to fail is less exposed to large-scale automation, even though its task-level exposure to AI may appear high.

\begin{example}
    Consider a job consisting of $m$ steps (with $m$ even) whose steps alternate between easy and hard for an AI to complete: odd-numbered steps succeed with probability $1$, while even-numbered steps succeed with probability only $0.1$.
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{cccc}
\toprule
Step ($i$) & \makecell{Manual\\$(\manualSkill{i}, \manualTime{i})$} & \makecell{AI mgmt.\\$(\AIskill{i}, \AItime{i})$} & \makecell{AI success\\prob.\ ($q_i$)} \\
\midrule
Odd $i$ (easy) & $(1, 5)$ & $(1, 1)$ & $1$ \\
Even $i$ (hard) & $(1, 5)$ & $(1, 1)$ & $0.1$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
    \input{plots/TikZ_visualization/example1_visualization.tex}
    In this scenario, it is suboptimal to attempt to use AI on any of the even-numbered steps, even as part of a chain.
    The optimal AI deployment strategy is to employ AI-augmentation on the $m/2$ odd-numbered steps (for a cost of $1$ each) and perform the $m/2$ even-numbered steps manually (for a cost of $5$ each), for an overall cost of
    \[
    \tfrac{m}{2}(1) + \tfrac{m}{2}(5) = \tfrac{m}{2} + \tfrac{5m}{2} = 3m.
    \]
\end{example}
\begin{example}
    Next suppose that easy and hard steps are not interleaved but clustered: the first $m/2$ steps each succeed with probability $1$ and the last $m/2$ steps each succeed with probability $0.1$.
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{cccc}
\toprule
Step ($i$) & \makecell{Manual\\$(\manualSkill{i}, \manualTime{i})$} & \makecell{AI mgmt.\\$(\AIskill{i}, \AItime{i})$} & \makecell{AI success\\prob.\ ($q_i$)} \\
\midrule
$i \le m/2$ (easy) & $(1, 5)$ & $(1, 1)$ & $1$ \\
$i > m/2$ (hard) & $(1, 5)$ & $(1, 1)$ & $0.1$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
    \input{plots/TikZ_visualization/example2_visualization.tex}
    In this case, the optimal AI strategy chains together the first $m/2$ steps into a single automated AI chain, for a combined cost of $1$, and performs the remaining $m/2$ steps manually (for a cost of $5$ each), for an overall cost of
    \[
    1 + \tfrac{m}{2}(5) = 1 + \tfrac{5m}{2},
    \]
    which is strictly less than the $3m$ of the interleaved case whenever $m \geq 4$.
\end{example}

To make this intuition more precise, let us return to the firm's deployment problem in \eqref{eq:totalcost_shortterm} and Section~\ref{sec:optimization.short}.
Consider the special case where AI management costs are normalized: $\AIskill{i}\AItime{i} = 1$ for all $i$.
That is, each step requires the same skill-weighted time to prompt and manage one attempt by an AI process.
Under this assumption, we will define a measure of the dispersion of AI-exposed steps in a production process, which we refer to as the \emph{fragmentation index} of a job.
We then show that the fragmentation index of a job approximates (up to constant factors) the cost of an optimal AI strategy for that job.
In other words, jobs for which the fragmentation index is high will tend to yield less benefit from AI automation.
Notably, this measure speaks to the chaining problem and abstracts from job design; we discuss how the bundling of tasks into jobs can overturn the intuition when describing long-run effects in Appendix~\ref{sec:discussion.skills}.

Intuitively, the fragmentation index is motivated by a hypothetical scenario where a prophet can ``see the future'' to determine which tasks would be completed successfully by AI on its first attempt and which would not. If many tasks in sequence will all complete successfully on the first try, these are a good candidate for an AI chain. The prophet might therefore chain together all contiguous sequences of tasks that would all succeed, and perform all other tasks manually. The fragmentation index is then the expected cost of this strategy, under the assumption that the prophet's foresight is correct. Notably, this AI strategy isn't necessarily optimal even with the benefit of foresight: a long AI chain with a good probability of success might be optimal even if the prophet knows that it will fail on the first attempt. However, what we show is that its cost approximates the cost of the optimal strategy even without foresight.

To define the fragmentation index more formally, consider a random process in which each step $s_i$ succeeds independently with probability $q_i$; any task that does not succeed is said to fail.  
Write $F$ for the set of steps that fail, and $\mathcal{C} = \{C_1, \dotsc, C_k\}$ for the random variable representing the collection of maximal connected components of non-failed steps.  
The weight of each $C_j \in \mathcal{C}$ is defined to be $\omega(C_j) = \min\{ 1, \sum_{i \in C_j} \manualSkill{i}\manualTime{i} \}$.
That is, each $C_j$ has weight $1$ unless the sum of the manual costs of its steps is less than $1$ (due to our assumption that AI management costs are normalized to $1$).

Given a realization of $\mathcal{C}$ and $F$, we define the realized fragmentation to be 
\begin{equation}
    \sum_{i \in F}\min\left\{\manualSkill{i}\manualTime{i}, \frac{\AIskill{i}\AItime{i}}{q_i}\right\} + \sum_{C_j \in \mathcal{C}} \omega(C_j).\label{eq:fragmentation}
\end{equation}
The \emph{fragmentation index} is defined to be the expected value of the realized fragmentation.

Intuitively, we expect the fragmentation index to be lower when highly automatable steps (that is, those for which the AI are likely to succeed) are clustered together, since a large cluster of highly automatable steps are more likely to realize as a single connected component when failures are realized.

We will show that the fragmentation index is within a constant factor of the cost of the optimal AI strategy for a given step sequence.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:fragmentation}
   Fix a sequence of $m$ steps $\mathcal{S} = \{s_1, \dotsc, s_m\}$, each with normalized AI management cost $\AIskill{i}\AItime{i} = 1$.
   Let $FI$ denote its fragmentation index and let $OPT$ denote the minimum cost over all AI strategies.
   Then $\tfrac{1}{8}OPT \leq FI \leq \tfrac{5}{4}OPT$.
   If we further assume $\manualSkill{i}\manualTime{i} \geq 1$ for all $i$, then $\tfrac{1}{4}OPT \leq FI \leq \tfrac{5}{4}OPT$.
\end{proposition}

We prove Proposition~\ref{prop:fragmentation} in Appendix~\ref{app:FI}.  
The key take-away from Proposition~\ref{prop:fragmentation} is that jobs with high fragmentation index---i.e., those for which the expected number of \emph{consecutive} successful step executions by an AI is low---will tend to have higher costs even under optimal use of AI chaining.
A key driver of efficiency gains via AI automation is therefore not simply the expected number of steps that can be automated effectively, but the number of \emph{adjacent} steps in the production process that have high exposure to AI.

An implication of our proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:fragmentation} is a natural and approximately optimal greedy algorithm for constructing AI strategies. The algorithm groups tasks into chains as long as the probability of success is sufficiently high; if the success probability falls too low then the chain is terminated and a new chain is started (with chains of length 1 converted to manual execution when appropriate).



\subsection{Non-Linear Impacts of AI Improvements}
\label{sec:nonlinear}

Improvements to AI technology naturally lead to improvements in the overall cost of production, but these effects need not be linear.  
The full economic impact of a disruptive general-purpose technology can take significant time and investment to materialize, with notable historical examples ranging from the steam engine to electricity to computers.  
In each case, a new technology enables modest short-term improvements to existing production processes, but the full impact is not felt until large-scale reorganization of production is enabled.  
This drives a potentially non-monotone marginal value from technology improvements, where major improvements are only unlocked after surpassing a threshold of capability at which they become feasible.

Our framework captures such non-monotonicities in the marginal value of technological improvements.  
This arises in our model via optimization over different AI deployment strategies.
One can think of parameter $\alpha \in (0,1]$ in our model as metric of the quality of a general purpose AI technology, taken to be the probability that a task of normalized difficulty $1$ is completed successfully.
We can then model general technology improvements as increasing the value of $\alpha$.
For a given \emph{fixed} AI strategy (described by a task sequence $\T$), the total cost can be expressed as a polynomial in $(1/\alpha)$.
As a result, the cost of $\T$ shifts in a continuous and smooth manner as $\alpha$ increases, with monotone marginal gains to technology improvements.
However, the firm optimizes over many possible AI strategies, expressed as the deployment problem \eqref{eq:totalcost_shortterm}, taking the minimum-cost solution.
AI strategies with a higher degree of AI chaining will naturally involve higher powers of $1/\alpha$ in their cost expressions, meaning that they are more sensitive to changes in $\alpha$ and become preferable only at higher values of $\alpha$ (i.e., as AI becomes more effective as a tool). 
This naturally leads to scenarios where the productivity improvements of AI integration are modest at low levels of quality but can grow sharply past a certain inflection point, as the following example illustrates.

\begin{example}
Consider a two-step process with unit skill, with the following step parameters:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{ccccc}
\toprule
Step ($i$) & \makecell{Manual\\$(\manualSkill{i}, \manualTime{i})$} & \makecell{AI mgmt.\\$(\AIskill{i}, \AItime{i})$} & \makecell{Step\\difficulty ($d_i$)} & \makecell{AI success\\prob.\ ($q_i$)} \\
\midrule
1 & $(1, 1)$ & $(1, 1)$ & $6$ & $\alpha^{6}$ \\
2 & $(1, 2)$ & $(1, 1)$ & $1$ & $\alpha^{1}$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
Step~1 is short but hard for AI, while step~2 is more time-consuming but easy; the AI succeeds at step~$i$ with probability $q_i = \alpha^{d_i}$.
The panels below trace the optimal cost (panel~a) and its marginal benefit (panel~b) as AI quality $\alpha$ rises.

\begin{center}
\begin{minipage}{0.49\linewidth}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{plots/example5_costs.png}\\
{\footnotesize (a) Total cost by AI strategy}
\end{minipage}\hfill
\begin{minipage}{0.49\linewidth}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{plots/example5_marginalBenefit.png}\\
{\footnotesize (b) Marginal benefit of improving $\alpha$}
\end{minipage}
\end{center}

Below $\alpha = 0.50$ both steps are manual; between $0.50$ and $0.90$ the firm augments step~2; and above $0.90$ it chains both steps, automating step~1:
\begin{align*}
\text{both manual:} \quad & \manualSkill{1}\manualTime{1} + \manualSkill{2}\manualTime{2} = (1)(1) + (1)(2) = 3, \\
\text{augment step 2:} \quad & \manualSkill{1}\manualTime{1} + \frac{\AIskill{2}\,\AItime{2}}{q_2} = (1)(1) + \frac{(1)(1)}{\alpha} = 1 + \tfrac{1}{\alpha}, \\
\text{chain (steps 1--2):} \quad & \frac{\AIskill{2}\,\AItime{2}}{q_1 q_2} = \frac{(1)(1)}{\alpha^{6}\,\alpha^{1}} = \alpha^{-7}.
\end{align*}
The marginal benefit of better AI jumps at each threshold, and---crucially---the jump at $\alpha \approx 0.90$ \emph{exceeds} the one at $\alpha \approx 0.50$ (panel~b): once chaining draws an additional step under AI, cost falls more steeply, so the returns to AI investment can rise as more of the workflow is automated.
\end{example}

Proposition~\ref{prop:reorg_threshold} formalizes this pattern for a production process with any number of steps and chains. The firm's cost is the lower envelope of the cost curves of all AI strategies---each curve a fixed manual cost plus a sum of chain terms $\AIskill{r_c}\AItime{r_c}\,\alpha^{-D_c}$, one per chain---and the returns to AI quality diminish within any fixed configuration but jump upward at each threshold where the firm reorganizes its chains.

\begin{proposition}[Reorganization thresholds and increasing returns to AI quality]
\label{prop:reorg_threshold}
For any AI strategy $\T$, total cost is
\[
C_\T(\alpha) = K_\T + \sum_{c \in \mathcal{C}(\T)} \AIskill{r_c}\AItime{r_c}\,\alpha^{-D_c},
\]
where $K_\T = \sum_{\text{manual } i} \manualSkill{i}\manualTime{i}$ is the fixed manual cost, $\mathcal{C}(\T)$ is the set of AI chains in $\T$, and chain $c$ has augmented endpoint $r_c$ and total difficulty $D_c = \sum_{i \in c} d_i \ge 1$. Let $C^*(\alpha) = \min_{\T} C_\T(\alpha)$ be the firm's optimal cost over $\alpha \in (0,1]$. Then:
\emph{(i)} each $C_\T$ is convex and non-increasing in $\alpha$, so $C^*$ is the lower envelope of finitely many convex non-increasing curves---continuous, non-increasing, and equal on each of finitely many maximal intervals to a single strategy's cost, with the all-manual strategy optimal as $\alpha \to 0^+$; and
\emph{(ii)} the marginal benefit of AI quality $g(\alpha) = -\,\mathrm{d}C^*/\mathrm{d}\alpha \ge 0$ is strictly decreasing within each regime (diminishing returns to a fixed configuration, and $g \equiv 0$ while no AI is used), yet jumps upward at every threshold where the optimal strategy changes.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
For each strategy, $C_\T'(\alpha) = -\sum_{c} \AIskill{r_c}\AItime{r_c}\,D_c\,\alpha^{-D_c-1} \le 0$ and $C_\T''(\alpha) = \sum_{c} \AIskill{r_c}\AItime{r_c}\,D_c(D_c+1)\,\alpha^{-D_c-2} \ge 0$, so $C_\T$ is non-increasing and convex (and constant exactly when $\T$ uses no chain). There are finitely many strategies, so $C^*$ is the minimum of their cost curves: continuous and non-increasing, and since any two distinct curves---polynomials in $1/\alpha$---agree at only finitely many points, the set of $\alpha$ at which the optimal strategy changes is finite. As $\alpha \to 0^+$ every chain term $\AIskill{r_c}\AItime{r_c}\,\alpha^{-D_c} \to \infty$ while the all-manual cost stays fixed, so the all-manual strategy is optimal near $0$. This proves~(i).

Within a regime where $\T$ is optimal, $g(\alpha) = -C_\T'(\alpha) = \sum_c \AIskill{r_c}\AItime{r_c}\,D_c\,\alpha^{-D_c-1} \ge 0$ is strictly decreasing in $\alpha$ (and zero if $\T$ uses no chain). At a threshold $\alpha_0$ where the optimum switches from $\T$ (optimal just below) to $\T'$ (optimal just above), the two costs are equal at $\alpha_0$, with $C_{\T'} \le C_\T$ for $\alpha > \alpha_0$ and $C_\T \le C_{\T'}$ for $\alpha < \alpha_0$. Hence $\phi = C_{\T'} - C_\T$ satisfies $\phi(\alpha_0) = 0$, $\phi \le 0$ above and $\phi \ge 0$ below, so $\phi'(\alpha_0) \le 0$, i.e.\ $-C_{\T'}'(\alpha_0) \ge -C_\T'(\alpha_0)$: the marginal benefit jumps up as $\alpha$ crosses $\alpha_0$. This proves~(ii).
\end{proof}

Two features drive the non-linearity. Within a fixed configuration the returns to AI quality diminish, as a given set of chains is simply run with fewer retries as $\alpha$ rises. The accelerations come at the reorganization thresholds, where the firm folds more steps into longer chains; because difficulties add linearly a longer chain carries a higher power of $1/\alpha$, hence a strictly steeper cost curve, and the marginal return jumps up as the firm moves onto it. Each threshold is gatekept by the fixed manual cost the reorganization displaces---a longer chain becomes worthwhile only once AI is reliable enough for its $\alpha^{-D}$ cost to fall below the manual work it removes. The two-step example traces out two such thresholds: augmenting step~2 begins at $\alpha \approx 0.50$ and chaining both steps takes over at $\alpha \approx 0.90$, with the marginal return jumping up at each.
