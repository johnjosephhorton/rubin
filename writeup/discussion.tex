\section{Discussion}

In this section we draw out three implications of the model.
First, we show that AI chaining can overturn comparative advantage logic: a step that is cheaper to perform manually in isolation may nonetheless be automated in the optimal configuration of production (Section~\ref{sec:comparative_advantage}).
Second, we introduce a \emph{fragmentation index} that captures how dispersed a job's AI-suitable steps are, and show that it approximates the cost of the firm's optimal AI strategy, so that the gains from AI depend on the arrangement of AI-able steps, not merely on what fraction of job's steps suitable for AI execution (Section~\ref{sec:fragmentation}).
Third, we show that the marginal gains from improving AI quality are non-monotone, abruptly rising whenever improvements in AI quality make redesigning workflows worthwhile (Section~\ref{sec:nonlinear}).
We collect the proofs of all propositions in this section in Appendix~\ref{app:omitted_proofs}.

\subsection{Comparative Advantage and AI Chaining}
\label{sec:comparative_advantage}

For a single step, the decision whether or not to deploy AI depends simply on whether the manual execution cost exceeds the AI augmentation cost.
With more than one step, however, the optimal deployment of AI depends on more than each step's own characteristics, because of the benefits of automating multiple steps together in an AI chain.
As a result, it is possible to have a step that is more cost-effective to perform manually be automated as part of a chain.
In other words, the factor of production holding the comparative advantage on a step \emph{in isolation} need not be the one that performs it in the optimal production configuration.

In what follows we show how comparative advantage can be overturned using simple examples before formally introducing the economic force pushing for it.

\begin{example}
Consider a two-step workflow with unit skill, so a task's cost equals its expected completion time, with the following step parameters:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{c|ccc}
\toprule
\makecell{Step\\$(i)$} & \makecell{Manual cost\\$(\manualSkill{i}, \manualTime{i})$} & \makecell{AI management cost\\$(\AIskill{i}, \AItime{i})$} & \makecell{AI success probability\\$(q_i)$} \\
\midrule
1 & $(1, 1)$ & $(1, 2)$ & $0.9$ \\
2 & $(1, 4)$ & $(1, 1)$ & $0.9$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
In isolation, step~1 is cheaper \emph{manually}: its manual cost
\[
\manualSkill{1}\manualTime{1} = (1)(1) = 1
\]
lies below its augmentation cost
\[
\frac{\AIskill{1}\AItime{1}}{q_1} = \frac{(1)(2)}{0.9} \approx 2.22,
\]
so the human holds the comparative advantage on step~1.
Step~2, by contrast, is cheaper with \emph{AI}: its augmentation cost
\[
\frac{\AIskill{2}\AItime{2}}{q_2} = \frac{(1)(1)}{0.9} \approx 1.11
\]
lies below its manual cost
\[
\manualSkill{2}\manualTime{2} = (1)(4) = 4.
\]
Comparative advantage thus designates step~1 for manual execution, yet the overall cost-minimizing strategy chains both steps and automates step~1.
The chain's cost,
\[
\frac{\AIskill{2}\,\AItime{2}}{q_1\, q_2} = \frac{(1)(1)}{(0.9)(0.9)} = \frac{1}{0.81} \approx 1.23,
\]
falls below the comparative advantage assignment (step~1 manual and step~2 augmented), which costs
\[
\manualSkill{1}\manualTime{1} + \frac{\AIskill{2}\,\AItime{2}}{q_2} = (1)(1) + \frac{(1)(1)}{0.9} \approx 1 + 1.11 = 2.11,
\]
and below every other strategy.
\input{plots/TikZ_visualization/comparative_advantage_visualization.tex}
The reason is automating step~1 inside the chain removes its direct cost, and implicitly substitutes it with a verification cost incurred for step~2, leaving only the joint-success penalty ($q_1 q_2 = 0.81$).
The human's comparative advantage on step~1 is thus overturned: in the optimal configuration the step is automated, at zero direct cost.
\end{example}

Proposition~\ref{prop:ca_local} formalizes the idea behind Example 1, which is that a human-advantaged step sitting just before an AI chain can be automated as soon as its \emph{marginal} contribution to the chain falls short of its manual execution cost.

\begin{proposition}[Local Overturning of Comparative Advantage]
\label{prop:ca_local}
Consider a step $k$ on which the human holds the comparative advantage in isolation, $\manualSkill{k}\manualTime{k} < \AIskill{k}\AItime{k}/q_k$, and any strategy in which the following steps $k+1, \dotsc, r$ are executed as an AI chain ending (augmented) at $r$ while step $k$ is performed manually.
If
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ca_local}
\underbrace{\frac{\AIskill{r}\AItime{r}\,(1-q_k)}{\prod_{i=k}^{r} q_i}}_{\text{marginal cost of folding step $k$ into the chain}} \;<\; \manualSkill{k}\manualTime{k},
\end{equation}
then the strategy in which step $k$ is executed manually is not cost-minimizing: automating step $k$ and extending the chain to $(k, \dotsc, r)$ is strictly cheaper.
\end{proposition}

The two-step example above instantiates Proposition~\ref{prop:ca_local}, with the chain consisting of the single augmented step~$2$:
\[
\frac{\AIskill{2}\AItime{2}\,(1-q_1)}{q_1\, q_2} = \frac{(1)(0.1)}{(0.9)(0.9)} \approx 0.12 \;<\; 1 = \manualSkill{1}\manualTime{1}.
\]

Next, we modify the example to show that even if appending the human-advantaged step alone to a chain is not optimal it is still possible to see it automated if an AI-friendly step precedes it.
First, lower the AI success probabilities of both steps in Example 1 so much so that chaining steps~1 and~2 together is no longer optimal.
Then place before step~1 a new step~0 that AI executes very reliably but that is expensive to carry out on its own, whether manually or as a standalone augmented step.
Because a chain pays only for the management of its augmented endpoint, such a step is cheapest to absorb as the start of a chain, where its own management cost goes unpaid.
And because chains are contiguous, the chain that step~0 anchors must run through step~1 to reach the cheap endpoint at step~2.
Step~1 is therefore pulled into the chain and automated, just as in the previous example, even though steps~1 and~2 on their own would no longer be chained together.

\begin{example}
Consider three consecutive steps with unit skill, so a task's cost equals its expected completion time, with the following step parameters:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{c|ccc}
\toprule
\makecell{Step\\$(i)$} & \makecell{Manual cost\\$(\manualSkill{i}, \manualTime{i})$} & \makecell{AI management cost\\$(\AIskill{i}, \AItime{i})$} & \makecell{AI success probability\\$(q_i)$} \\
\midrule
0 & $(1, 5)$ & $(1, 4)$ & $0.95$ \\
1 & $(1, 1)$ & $(1, 2)$ & $0.50$ \\
2 & $(1, 4)$ & $(1, 1)$ & $0.80$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
In isolation, step~1 is cheaper \emph{manually}: its manual cost
\[
\manualSkill{1}\manualTime{1} = (1)(1) = 1
\]
lies below its augmentation cost
\[
\frac{\AIskill{1}\AItime{1}}{q_1} = \frac{(1)(2)}{0.5} = 4,
\]
so the human holds the comparative advantage on step~1.

Take steps~1 and~2 on their own first (the top row of the figure).
Performing step~1 manually and augmenting step~2 costs
\[
\manualSkill{1}\manualTime{1} + \frac{\AIskill{2}\AItime{2}}{q_2} = 1 + \frac{(1)(1)}{0.8} = 1 + 1.25 = 2.25,
\]
which is below the cost of chaining the two,
\[
\frac{\AIskill{2}\AItime{2}}{q_1\, q_2} = \frac{(1)(1)}{(0.5)(0.8)} = \frac{1}{0.4} = 2.5.
\]
Absent the predecessor, then, comparative advantage holds and step~1 is performed manually.
\input{plots/TikZ_visualization/predecessor_visualization.tex}

Now introduce the predecessor, step~0 (the bottom row of the figure).
It is reliable for AI ($q_0 = 0.95$) but expensive to execute on its own in either mode: performing it manually costs
\[
\manualSkill{0}\manualTime{0} = (1)(5) = 5,
\]
while augmenting it as a standalone step costs
\[
\frac{\AIskill{0}\AItime{0}}{q_0} = \frac{(1)(4)}{0.95} \approx 4.21.
\]
The only cheap way to absorb step~0 is to place it at the start of a chain, where its management cost goes unpaid.
The cost-minimizing strategy therefore chains all three steps into a single AI chain augmented at step~2, at cost
\[
\frac{\AIskill{2}\AItime{2}}{q_0\, q_1\, q_2} = \frac{(1)(1)}{(0.95)(0.5)(0.8)} = \frac{1}{0.38} \approx 2.63.
\]
This falls far below the cheapest arrangement that keeps step~1 manual, which executes each step separately on its own,
\[
\min\!\left\{\manualSkill{0}\manualTime{0},\ \frac{\AIskill{0}\AItime{0}}{q_0}\right\} + \manualSkill{1}\manualTime{1} + \min\!\left\{\manualSkill{2}\manualTime{2},\ \frac{\AIskill{2}\AItime{2}}{q_2}\right\} \approx 4.21 + 1 + 1.25 = 6.46,
\]
and below every other strategy.
Step~1, which on its own would be performed manually, is automated only to bridge the AI-friendly predecessor to the cheap endpoint at step~2.
Its own success probability is just $q_1 = 0.5$, so the chain frequently fails and is retried; the firm accepts this because handling step~0 in any other way is far more costly.
\end{example}

Proposition~\ref{prop:ca_predecessor} formalizes the idea introduced in Example 2, showing that an AI-reliable but expensive predecessor might pull a human-advantaged step into a chain by putting pressure on it from the other side.

\begin{proposition}[Overturning by an AI-Friendly Predecessor]
\label{prop:ca_predecessor}
Consider a step $k$ on which the human holds the comparative advantage in isolation, $\manualSkill{k}\manualTime{k} < \AIskill{k}\AItime{k}/q_k$, and any strategy in which step $k$ is performed manually, its predecessor $k-1$ is executed on its own (manually or as a standalone augmented step), and its successor $k+1$ is executed as a standalone augmented step.
Suppose the successor is AI-attractive while chaining step $k$ with it alone does not pay,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pred_local}
\frac{\AIskill{k+1}\AItime{k+1}}{q_{k+1}} < \manualSkill{k+1}\manualTime{k+1}
\qquad\text{and}\qquad
\underbrace{\frac{\AIskill{k+1}\AItime{k+1}\,(1-q_k)}{q_k\, q_{k+1}}}_{\text{marginal cost of folding step $k$ in alone}} \ge \manualSkill{k}\manualTime{k},
\end{equation}
so that, were the predecessor absent, step $k$ would indeed be performed manually.
If the marginal cost of folding the pair $\{k-1, k\}$ into the chain falls below the cost of running the pair on its own,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:pred_chain}
\underbrace{\frac{\AIskill{k+1}\AItime{k+1}\,(1-q_{k-1}q_k)}{q_{k-1}\,q_k\,q_{k+1}}}_{\text{marginal cost of folding the pair }\{k-1,k\}\text{ in}}
\;<\;
\underbrace{\min\!\left\{\manualSkill{k-1}\manualTime{k-1},\ \frac{\AIskill{k-1}\AItime{k-1}}{q_{k-1}}\right\}}_{\text{cheapest standalone }k-1}
\;+\;
\underbrace{\manualSkill{k}\manualTime{k}}_{\text{manual }k},
\end{equation}
then the strategy is not cost-minimizing: running $k-1$, $k$, $k+1$ as a single AI chain augmented at $k+1$ is strictly cheaper.
The human-advantaged step $k$ is thus automated in the cheaper arrangement, drawn into a chain begun by the predecessor step $k-1$.
\end{proposition}

Proposition~\ref{prop:ca_predecessor} reads as Proposition~\ref{prop:ca_local} with the unit folded into the chain enlarged from a single step to the contiguous pair $\{k-1, k\}$.
Because the chain ending augmented at $k+1$ pays only for its endpoint, absorbing the pair costs just the endpoint's management inflated by the pair's joint failure risk $1 - q_{k-1}q_k$, and nothing to manage either step inside it.
The reversal occurs when this one marginal cost falls below the price of running the pair the old way: the predecessor optimally on its own plus step $k$ manually.

Decomposing the pair's joint failure risk as $1 - q_{k-1}q_k = (1-q_{k-1}) + q_{k-1}(1-q_k)$ splits the marginal cost of folding the pair into a separate contribution from each step,
\[
\underbrace{\frac{\AIskill{k+1}\AItime{k+1}\,(1-q_{k-1}q_k)}{q_{k-1}\,q_k\,q_{k+1}}}_{\text{marginal cost of folding the pair $\{k-1, k\}$}}
\;=\;
\underbrace{\frac{\AIskill{k+1}\AItime{k+1}\,(1-q_{k-1})}{q_{k-1}\,q_k\,q_{k+1}}}_{\text{step $k-1$'s share}}
\;+\;
\underbrace{\frac{\AIskill{k+1}\AItime{k+1}\,(1-q_k)}{q_k\,q_{k+1}}}_{\text{step $k$'s share}}.
\]
Step $k$'s share is exactly the cost of folding it in alone, which by~\eqref{eq:pred_local} already exceeds its manual cost, so step $k$ is not pulled under AI on its own merits.
The predecessor's share is governed by $1 - q_{k-1}$ alone, which shrinks toward zero as the predecessor becomes reliable for AI, even as its standalone execution cost stays large.
The reversal therefore runs entirely through the predecessor: its near-zero marginal term, set against the large standalone cost it displaces, is what covers step $k$'s shortfall and pulls the human-advantaged step under AI.

Example~2 above is an illustration of this. 
The marginal cost of folding the pair $\{0, 1\}$ into the chain ending at step~2 is
\[
\frac{\AIskill{2}\AItime{2}\,(1-q_0 q_1)}{q_0\, q_1\, q_2} = \frac{(1)\bigl(1 - (0.95)(0.5)\bigr)}{(0.95)(0.5)(0.8)} \approx 1.38 \;<\; 5.21 = \min\!\left\{\manualSkill{0}\manualTime{0},\ \frac{\AIskill{0}\AItime{0}}{q_0}\right\} + \manualSkill{1}\manualTime{1},
\]
of which the predecessor's share, $\AIskill{2}\AItime{2}(1-q_0)/(q_0 q_1 q_2) \approx 0.13$, is dwarfed by the $\approx 4.21$ its cheapest standalone execution would cost.

We must emphasize that both Proposition~\ref{prop:ca_local} and Proposition~\ref{prop:ca_predecessor} are only local statements in the sense that they hold the execution mode of the rest of steps in production sequence fixed.
Whether comparative advantage is overturned in the optimal arrangement of the entire production sequence is the more demanding question, because which strategy minimizes cost overall depends not just on the human-advantaged step and its neighbors but on how the whole workflow is organized.

Proposition~\ref{prop:ca_threshold} addresses this dependence and gives a sufficient condition under which step $k$ is automated in \emph{every} cost-minimizing strategy.\footnote{This argument relies on a most-favorable scenario rather than the least demanding sufficient condition, since our aim is only to convey the intuition for why classic comparative advantage logic can break down under fairly mild conditions.}
The idea behind this proposition is a simple extension of the logic used in Proposition~\ref{prop:ca_local}.
Consider two ways of handling the workflow from step $k$ onward: chaining all of steps $k, \dotsc, m$ into a single AI chain, or performing steps $k$ and $m$ separately, each optimally on its own. 
Whenever the chain is cheaper of the two, step $k$ is automated in every optimum, regardless of how the intervening steps are arranged. 
The reason is that any strategy keeping step $k$ manual can do no better than performing $k$ and $m$ separately and optimally. 
So once a single chain over the whole stretch undercuts even that benchmark, leaving step $k$ manual can never be cost-minimizing.

\begin{proposition}[Sufficient Condition for Overturning Comparative Advantage]
\label{prop:ca_threshold}
Consider any workflow with steps $s_1, \dotsc, s_m$ and a step $k < m$ on which the human holds the comparative advantage in isolation, $\manualSkill{k}\manualTime{k} < \AIskill{k}\AItime{k}/q_k$.
If
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:ca_sufficient}
\underbrace{\frac{\AIskill{m}\AItime{m}}{\prod_{i=k}^{m} q_i}}_{\substack{\text{cost of AI chain}\\\text{over steps }k,\dotsc,m}}
\;<\;
\underbrace{\manualSkill{k}\manualTime{k}}_{\substack{\text{manual cost}\\\text{of step }k}}
\;+\;
\underbrace{\min\!\left\{\manualSkill{m}\manualTime{m},\ \frac{\AIskill{m}\AItime{m}}{q_m}\right\}}_{\substack{\text{cheapest execution}\\\text{of final step }m}},
\end{equation}
then no optimal strategy assigns step $k$ by comparative advantage: performing step $k$ manually is strictly suboptimal, so step $k$ is automated by AI in the optimal arrangement.
\end{proposition}

Unlike Proposition~\ref{prop:ca_local}, condition~\eqref{eq:ca_sufficient} makes no assumption about how the rest of the sequence is organized, and this generality is its strength.
It chains the whole tail $k, \dotsc, m$ but credits back $\min\{\manualSkill{m}\manualTime{m},\, \AIskill{m}\AItime{m}/q_m\}$, the cost the terminal step must incur under any strategy: a chain reaching step $m$ pays for the verification cost of its final output regardless of step $k$, so this is not a true extra burden of folding step $k$ in.
On the two-step workflow in Example 1 above, $m = 2$ and the credited-back floor is exactly $\AIskill{2}\AItime{2}/q_2$, so \eqref{eq:ca_sufficient} collapses to the local margin~\eqref{eq:ca_local} and the reversal is guaranteed, even though the raw chain cost $1/0.81 \approx 1.23$ exceeds $\manualSkill{1}\manualTime{1} = 1$.
The one price of the unconditional form is that the chain is anchored to the terminal step $m$, so it can miss reversals an interior endpoint $r < m$ would deliver, for instance when a low-success step late in the workflow shrinks $\prod_{i=k}^{m} q_i$ and inflates the chain's cost but nonetheless automating step $k$; the conclusion also holds whenever the full-chain cost at some interior $r > k$ already undercuts $\manualSkill{k}\manualTime{k}$.

The broader takeaways is whether a step is automated depends not just on its own cost parameters (as comparative advantage logic would predict) but also on characteristics of its neighbors.
The pressure to fold a human-advantaged step into a chain can come from either an AI-reliable predecessor \emph{upstream} (Proposition~\ref{prop:ca_predecessor}) or a cheap chain endpoint \emph{downstream} (Propositions~\ref{prop:ca_local} and \ref{prop:ca_threshold}).
Either way, a human-advantaged step sitting beside an AI-suitable run might be drawn into a chain and automated due to cost savings available in its local work context, whereas the same step sitting beside steps that AI completes only with low probability is likely to remain manual.
A step with identical characteristics can therefore be automated in one workflow yet performed manually in another, depending only on the AI-suitability of its neighbors.
This is precisely the variation we exploit in Section~\ref{sec:DWA_prediction} to show that AI execution likelihood of a step depends on its local work context in different occupations.



\subsection{Job-level AI Exposure and the Fragmentation Index}
\label{sec:fragmentation}

Returning to the firm's AI deployment problem described in \eqref{eq:totalcost_shortterm} and Section~\ref{sec:optimization.short}, we can interpret job-level AI exposure as the extent to which an optimal AI strategy employs AI automation and augmentation.
Intuitively, a run of consecutive steps in the workflow that an AI can perform effectively is a natural candidate for an AI chain.
A job that contains such runs of consecutive steps is therefore likely to benefit most from AI automation.
On the other hand, a job for which AI-friendly steps are separated by intermediate steps that an AI is likely to fail is less exposed to large-scale automation, even though its step-level exposure to AI may appear high. 
The two examples below showcase this point.

\begin{example}
\label{ex:fragmentation_interleaved}
Consider a job of $m$ steps (with $m$ even) with unit skill, so a task's cost equals its expected completion time.
The steps alternate between \emph{easy} and \emph{hard} for AI: odd-numbered steps succeed with probability $q_i = 1$, while even-numbered steps succeed with probability only $q_i = 0.1$.
All steps share the costs below.
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{c|ccc}
\toprule
\makecell{Step\\$(i)$} & \makecell{Manual cost\\$(\manualSkill{i}, \manualTime{i})$} & \makecell{AI management cost\\$(\AIskill{i}, \AItime{i})$} & \makecell{AI success probability\\$(q_i)$} \\
\midrule
Odd $i$ (easy) & $(1, 5)$ & $(1, 1)$ & $1$ \\
Even $i$ (hard) & $(1, 5)$ & $(1, 1)$ & $0.1$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\vspace{0.25cm}

\input{plots/TikZ_visualization/example1_visualization.tex}

Because every easy step is flanked by hard ones, no two AI-friendly steps are ever adjacent, so no chain longer than a single step can form, and automation, which executes a step for free but is available only inside a chain, never becomes worthwhile.
The firm therefore treats each step on its own.
An easy step is cheaper augmented than performed manually, since $\AIskill{i}\AItime{i}/q_i = 1/1 = 1$ falls below $\manualSkill{i}\manualTime{i} = 5$, whereas a hard step is cheaper performed manually, since its manual cost $5$ falls below its augmentation cost $\AIskill{i}\AItime{i}/q_i = 1/0.1 = 10$.
The optimal strategy thus augments the $m/2$ easy steps (cost $1$ each) and performs the $m/2$ hard steps manually (cost $5$ each), for a total cost of
\[
\tfrac{m}{2}(1) + \tfrac{m}{2}(5) = 3m.
\]
\end{example}
\begin{example}
\label{ex:fragmentation_clustered}
Consider the same set of steps as in Example~\ref{ex:fragmentation_interleaved}, but now cluster the AI-friendly steps: the first $m/2$ steps are easy (each succeeds with probability $q_i = 1$) and the last $m/2$ are hard (each succeeds with probability $q_i = 0.1$).
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{c|ccc}
\toprule
\makecell{Step\\$(i)$} & \makecell{Manual cost\\$(\manualSkill{i}, \manualTime{i})$} & \makecell{AI management cost\\$(\AIskill{i}, \AItime{i})$} & \makecell{AI success probability\\$(q_i)$} \\
\midrule
$i \le m/2$ (easy) & $(1, 5)$ & $(1, 1)$ & $1$ \\
$i > m/2$ (hard) & $(1, 5)$ & $(1, 1)$ & $0.1$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\vspace{0.25cm}

\input{plots/TikZ_visualization/example2_visualization.tex}

Now the easy steps form a contiguous run, so the firm can chain them: it automates the first $m/2 - 1$ at zero direct cost and verifies only once, at the end of the run.
Because every easy step succeeds on the first attempt, the entire run of $m/2$ easy steps collapses to the single management cost of its augmented endpoint,
\[
\frac{\AIskill{m/2}\,\AItime{m/2}}{\prod_{i=1}^{m/2} q_i} = \frac{1}{1} = 1.
\]
The $m/2$ hard steps remain cheaper performed manually (cost $5$ each), for a total cost of
\[
1 + \tfrac{m}{2}(5) = 1 + \tfrac{5m}{2},
\]
strictly below the $3m$ of the interleaved arrangement whenever $m \geq 4$.
The same steps thus yield very different automation outcomes depending only on whether the AI-friendly ones are clustered or dispersed.
\end{example}

To make this intuition more precise, let us return to the firm's deployment problem in \eqref{eq:totalcost_shortterm} and Section~\ref{sec:optimization.short}.
Consider the special case where AI management costs are normalized: $\AIskill{i}\AItime{i} = 1$ for all $i$.
That is, each step requires the same skill-weighted time to prompt and manage one attempt by the AI.
Under this assumption, we will define a measure of the dispersion of AI-exposed steps in a workflow, which we refer to as the \emph{fragmentation index} of a job.
We then show that the fragmentation index of a job approximates (up to constant factors) the cost of an optimal AI strategy for that job.
In other words, jobs for which the fragmentation index is high will tend to yield less benefit from AI automation.
Notably, this measure speaks to the chaining problem and abstracts from job design; we discuss how the bundling of tasks into jobs can overturn the intuition when describing long-run effects in Appendix~\ref{sec:discussion.skills}.

Intuitively, the fragmentation index is motivated by a hypothetical scenario where a prophet can ``see the future'' to determine which tasks would be completed successfully by AI on its first attempt and which would not.
If many tasks in sequence will all complete successfully on the first try, these are a good candidate for an AI chain.
The prophet might therefore chain together all contiguous sequences of tasks that would all succeed, and perform all other tasks manually.
The fragmentation index is then the expected cost of this strategy, under the assumption that the prophet's foresight is correct.
Notably, this AI strategy isn't necessarily optimal even with the benefit of foresight: a long AI chain with a good probability of success might be optimal even if the prophet knows that it will fail on the first attempt.
However, what we show is that its cost approximates the cost of the optimal strategy even without foresight.

To define the fragmentation index more formally, suppose that each step $s_i$ succeeds independently with probability $q_i$; any task that does not succeed is said to fail.
Write $F$ for the set of steps that fail, and $\mathcal{C} = \{C_1, \dotsc, C_k\}$ for the random variable representing the collection of maximal connected components of non-failed steps.
The weight of each $C_j \in \mathcal{C}$ is defined to be $\omega(C_j) = \min\{ 1, \sum_{i \in C_j} \manualSkill{i}\manualTime{i} \}$.
That is, each $C_j$ has weight $1$ unless the sum of the manual costs of its steps is less than $1$ (due to our assumption that AI management costs are normalized to $1$).

Given a realization of $\mathcal{C}$ and $F$, we define the realized fragmentation to be
\begin{equation}
    \sum_{i \in F}\min\left\{\manualSkill{i}\manualTime{i}, \frac{\AIskill{i}\AItime{i}}{q_i}\right\} + \sum_{C_j \in \mathcal{C}} \omega(C_j).\label{eq:fragmentation}
\end{equation}
The \emph{fragmentation index} is defined to be the expected value of the realized fragmentation.

Intuitively, we expect the fragmentation index to be lower when highly automatable steps (that is, those for which the AI are likely to succeed) are clustered together, since a large cluster of highly automatable steps are more likely to realize as a single connected component when failures are realized.

We will show that the fragmentation index is within a constant factor of the cost of the optimal AI strategy for a given step sequence.

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:fragmentation}
   Fix a sequence of $m$ steps $\mathcal{S} = \{s_1, \dotsc, s_m\}$, each with normalized AI management cost $\AIskill{i}\AItime{i} = 1$.
   Let $FI$ denote its fragmentation index and let $OPT$ denote the minimum cost over all AI strategies.
   Then $\tfrac{1}{8}OPT \leq FI \leq \tfrac{5}{4}OPT$.
   If we further assume $\manualSkill{i}\manualTime{i} \geq 1$ for all $i$, then $\tfrac{1}{4}OPT \leq FI \leq \tfrac{5}{4}OPT$.
\end{proposition}

The key take-away from Proposition~\ref{prop:fragmentation} is that jobs with high fragmentation index (i.e., those for which the expected number of \emph{consecutive} successful step executions by an AI is low) will tend to have higher costs even under optimal use of AI chaining.
A key driver of efficiency gains via AI automation is therefore not simply the expected number of steps that can be automated effectively, but the number of \emph{adjacent} steps in the workflow that have high exposure to AI.

An implication of our proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:fragmentation} is a natural and approximately optimal greedy algorithm for constructing AI strategies.
The algorithm groups tasks into chains as long as the probability of success is sufficiently high; if the success probability falls too low then the chain is terminated and a new chain is started (with chains of length 1 converted to manual execution when appropriate).



\subsection{Non-Monotone Gains from AI Improvements}
\label{sec:nonlinear}

Improvements to AI technology naturally lead to improvements in the overall cost of production, but these effects need not be monotone.
The full economic impact of a disruptive general-purpose technology can take significant time and investment to materialize, with notable historical examples ranging from the steam engine to electricity to computers.
In each case, a new technology enables modest short-term improvements to existing workflows, but the full impact is not felt until large-scale reorganization of production is enabled \citep{brynjolfsson2021productivity}.
This drives a potentially non-monotone marginal value from technology improvements, where major improvements are only unlocked after surpassing a threshold of capability at which they become feasible.

Our framework captures such non-monotonicities in the marginal value of technological improvements.
This arises in our model via optimization over different AI deployment strategies.
One can think of parameter $\alpha \in (0,1]$ in our model as metric of the quality of a general purpose AI technology, taken to be the probability that a task of normalized difficulty $1$ is completed successfully.
We can then consider general technology improvements as increasing the value of $\alpha$.
Example below shows why gains from increasing AI quality need not be monotone.

\begin{example}
Consider a two-step workflow with unit skill, with the following step parameters:
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{c|cccc}
\toprule
\makecell{Step\\$(i)$} & \makecell{Manual cost\\$(\manualSkill{i}, \manualTime{i})$} & \makecell{AI management cost\\$(\AIskill{i}, \AItime{i})$} & \makecell{Step difficulty\\$(d_i)$} & \makecell{AI success probability\\$(q_i)$} \\
\midrule
1 & $(1, 1)$ & $(1, 1)$ & $6$ & $\alpha^{6}$ \\
2 & $(1, 2)$ & $(1, 1)$ & $1$ & $\alpha^{1}$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}
Step~1 is short but hard for AI, while step~2 is more time-consuming but easy; the AI succeeds at step~$i$ with probability $q_i = \alpha^{d_i}$.
The panels below trace the optimal cost (Panel~a) and its marginal benefit (Panel~b) as AI quality $\alpha$ rises.

\begin{center}
\begin{minipage}{0.49\linewidth}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{plots/example5_costs.png}\\
{\footnotesize (a) Total cost by AI strategy}
\end{minipage}\hfill
\begin{minipage}{0.49\linewidth}
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{plots/example5_marginalBenefit.png}\\
{\footnotesize (b) Marginal benefit of improving $\alpha$}
\end{minipage}
\end{center}

Below $\alpha = 0.50$ both steps are manual; between $\alpha = 0.50$ and $\alpha \approx 0.90$ the firm augments step~2; and above $\alpha \approx 0.90$ it chains both steps, automating step~1:
\begin{align*}
\text{both manual:} \quad & \manualSkill{1}\manualTime{1} + \manualSkill{2}\manualTime{2} = (1)(1) + (1)(2) = 3, \\
\text{augment step 2:} \quad & \manualSkill{1}\manualTime{1} + \frac{\AIskill{2}\,\AItime{2}}{q_2} = (1)(1) + \frac{(1)(1)}{\alpha} = 1 + \tfrac{1}{\alpha}, \\
\text{chain (steps 1--2):} \quad & \frac{\AIskill{2}\,\AItime{2}}{q_1 q_2} = \frac{(1)(1)}{\alpha^{6}\,\alpha^{1}} = \alpha^{-7}.
\end{align*}
The marginal benefit of improving AI quality is the negative derivative of cost, $-\mathrm{d}C/\mathrm{d}\alpha$, which across the three regimes is
\begin{align*}
\text{both manual:} \quad & -\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}\alpha}\,(3) = 0, \\
\text{augment step 2:} \quad & -\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}\alpha}\!\left(1 + \frac{1}{\alpha}\right) = \frac{1}{\alpha^{2}}, \\
\text{chain (steps 1--2):} \quad & -\frac{\mathrm{d}}{\mathrm{d}\alpha}\,\alpha^{-7} = \frac{7}{\alpha^{8}}.
\end{align*}
Panel~(b) shows that the marginal return to improving AI quality jumps up at each threshold where the optimal strategy switches, since a different strategy governs cost on either side of the threshold.
Moreover, the jump at $\alpha \approx 0.90$ is larger than the one at $\alpha = 0.50$: forming the chain pulls a second step under AI, so cost falls more steeply.
This ordering is specific to this example, however, since a later threshold need not introduce a longer chain than an earlier one.
\end{example}

To make the argument precise, consider a workflow with any number of steps and chains.
For any AI strategy $\T$, total cost is a fixed manual term plus a polynomial in $1/\alpha$,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:strategy_cost}
C_\T(\alpha) = K_\T + \sum_{c \in \mathcal{C}(\T)} \AIskill{r_c}\AItime{r_c}\,\alpha^{-D_c},
\end{equation}
where $K_\T = \sum_{\text{manual } i} \manualSkill{i}\manualTime{i}$ is the fixed cost of manually-executed steps and the sum runs over the AI chains of $\T$, denoted $\mathcal{C}(\T)$, with each chain $c$ (with augmented endpoint $r_c$) contributing a term of degree equal to its total difficulty $D_c = \sum_{i \in c} d_i \ge 1$.
The cost of $\T$ thus shifts in a continuous and smooth manner as $\alpha$ increases, with monotone marginal gains to technology improvements.

The firm optimizes costs over many possible AI strategies, expressed as the deployment problem \eqref{eq:totalcost_shortterm}. 
At each level of AI quality $\alpha$ the firm adopts the cheapest available strategy, so its optimal cost is the lower envelope of the strategy-specific cost curves, $C^*(\alpha) = \min_{\T} C_\T(\alpha)$.
We write $g_\T(\alpha) = -\,\mathrm{d}C_\T/\mathrm{d}\alpha$ for the marginal benefit of improving AI quality under a fixed strategy $\T$, and $g^*(\alpha) = -\,\mathrm{d}C^*/\mathrm{d}\alpha \ge 0$ for the firm's marginal benefit at the optimum; the function $g^*(\alpha)$ equals $g_\T(\alpha)$ on the regime of $\alpha$ values where $\T$ is optimal.
As $\alpha$ rises, the optimal strategy changes at finitely many values of $\alpha$, which we refer to as \emph{reorganization thresholds}, that separate one regime from the next.
Proposition~\ref{prop:reorg_threshold} below formalizes that although $g^*$ diminishes within each regime it jumps upward at every reorganization threshold, so the returns to improving AI quality are non-monotone.

\begin{proposition}[Non-monotone Marginal Returns to AI Quality Improvements]
\label{prop:reorg_threshold}
Let $C^*(\alpha) = \min_{\T} C_\T(\alpha)$ be the firm's optimal cost over all feasible AI strategies and $g^*(\alpha) = -\,\mathrm{d}C^*/\mathrm{d}\alpha$ its marginal benefit of improving AI quality.
Within each regime (an interval of $\alpha$ on which a single AI strategy is optimal), $g^*$ is non-increasing.
Moreover, at every reorganization threshold (where the optimal AI strategy changes), $g^*$ jumps upward.
\end{proposition}
