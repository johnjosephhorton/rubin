To make this intuition more precise, consider the short-run optimization problem described in \eqref{eq:totalcost_shortterm} and Section~\ref{sec:optimization.short} where the goal is to design an AI strategy that optimizes the time cost of production for a single job assigned to a single worker with fixed wage.
%Recall that in this short-run problem we are fixing a single job assigned to a worker with a fixed wage, so our focus is on optimizing the time cost of production.  
We'll focus on the case where AI management costs are normalized to $\AItime{i} = 1$, meaning that, while steps can differ in their task-level exposure to AI, the time needed to manage an AI process is identical across steps.
Under this assumption, we will define a measure of the dispersion of AI-exposed tasks in a production process, which we refer to as the \emph{fragmentation index} of a job.  
We then show that the fragmentation index of a job approximates (up to constant factors) the time cost of an optimal AI strategy for that job.  
In other words, jobs for which the fragmentation index is high will tend to yield less benefit from AI automation in the short-term where worker wages and job boundaries are fixed.
Notably, this intuition makes heavy use of the short-run assumption that worker wages are fixed and independent of task skill requirements; we discuss examples where this intuition fails when describing long-run effects in Section~\ref{sec:discussion.skills}.

Our definition of fragmentation index is motivated by the following hypothetical scenario. The AI strategy is being designed by a prophet who can ``see the future'' to determine which tasks would be completed successfully by AI on the very first attempt and which would not. Armed with this foresight, the prophet could chain together all contiguous sequences of tasks that would succeed and perform all other tasks manually. The fragmentation index is then the expected cost of this AI strategy, under the assumption that the prophet's foresight is correct. 

Notably, this AI strategy isn't necessarily optimal even with the benefit of foresight: a long AI chain with a good probability of success might be optimal even if the prophet knows that it will fail on the first attempt, due to its likelihood to succeed on a second or third attempt. However, what we will show is that the fragmentation index approximates the cost of the optimal strategy even without foresight. Furthermore, it is a measure of the dispersion of highly automatable tasks: when the tasks most likely to succeed are contiguous, the prophet's foresight allows it to safely construct larger chains on average and thereby achieve lower cost.

To define the fragmentation index more formally, fix the step sequence $\mathcal{S}$ and consider a random process in which each step $s_i$ succeeds independently with probability $q_i$. Any task that does not succeed is said to fail.  
Write $F$ for the set of steps that fail, and $\mathcal{C} = \{C_1, \dotsc, C_k\}$ for the random variable representing the collection of maximal connected components of non-failed steps.
The weight of each $C_j \in \mathcal{C}$ is defined to be $\omega(C_j) = \min\{ 1, \sum_{i \in C_j} \manualTime{i} \}$.  
That is, each $C_j$ has weight $1$ unless the sum of the manual time costs for each of its steps is less than $1$ (motivated by the normalization of AI management costs to $1$).
As a final piece of notation, for each $s_i$, write $\minTime{i} = \min\left\{\manualTime{i}, \frac{\AItime{i}}{q_i}\right\}$.  That is, $\minTime{i}$ is the minimum cost to implement step $s_i$ individually (whether manually or through AI augmentation).  

\begin{definition}
Given a step sequence $\mathcal{S}$, its \emph{fragmentation index} $FI(\mathcal{S})$ is 
\[ E\left[\sum_{s_i \in F}\minTime{i} + \sum_{C_j \in \mathcal{C}} \omega(C_j) \right] \]
where the expectation is over the realization of failed steps $F$ and collection $\mathcal{C}$ of maximal contiguous runs of non-failed steps, as described above.
\end{definition}

\begin{proposition}
\label{prop:fragmentation}
   Fix a single job with a sequence of $m$ steps $\mathcal{S} = \{s_1, \dotsc, s_m\}$, each with $\AItime{i} = 1$.
   Let $FI$ denote its fragmentation index and let $OPT$ denote the minimum time cost over all AI strategies.
   Then $\tfrac{1}{8}OPT \leq FI \leq \tfrac{5}{4}OPT$.
   If we further assume $\manualTime{i} \geq 1$ for all $i$, then $\tfrac{1}{4}OPT \leq FI \leq \tfrac{5}{4}OPT$.
\end{proposition}

The key take-away from Proposition~\ref{prop:fragmentation} is that jobs with high fragmentation index---i.e., those for which the expected number of \emph{consecutive} successful step executions by an AI is low---will tend to have higher time costs even under optimal use of AI chaining.  
A key driver of efficiency gains via AI automation is not simply the expected number of steps that can be automated effectively, but the number of \emph{adjacent} steps in the production process that have high exposure to AI.

A further implication of our proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:fragmentation} is a natural and approximately optimal greedy algorithm for constructing AI strategies. The algorithm groups steps into chains as long as the probability of success is sufficiently high; if the success probability falls too low then the chain is terminated and a new chain is started (with chains of length 1 converted to manual execution when appropriate). We find this greedy algorithm interesting not because of its computational implications --- since we already know from Proposition~\ref{prop:short_run_optimization} how to compute the optimal AI strategy --- but rather as a practical heuristic and source of intuition for what makes step sequences more or less exposed to large-scale AI automation.

We now highlight some key ideas and intuition from the proof of  Proposition~\ref{prop:fragmentation}.  The first step of the proof is to show that the fragmentation index is not much larger than $OPT$.  As noted earlier, this is not immediate: even though our prophet has the extra benefit of foresight in our motivating interpretation, they are restricted to using an AI strategy in which every AI chain completes successfully on its first attempt.  Indeed, the following simple example shows that the fragmentation index could be as high as $\tfrac{5}{4}OPT$.

\begin{example}
Suppose a job has 3 steps, $\mathcal{S} = \{s_1, s_2, s_3\}$. The first and last always succeed, $q_1 = q_3 = 1$, and the second step succeeds with probability $q_2 = 0.5$ and has a manual cost of $\manualTime{2} = 2$.
The optimal AI strategy automates all three steps into a single AI chain for an expected cost of $1/q_2 = 2$. The fragmentation index realizes to $1+2+1=4$ if the middle step fails, or $1$ if the middle step succeeds, yielding $FI = q_2 \times 1 + (1-q_2) \times (1+2+1) = 5/2$.
\end{example}

As it turns out, the bound of $5/4$ from this example is tight: for all task sequences,
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:FI.upperbound}
    FI \leq \tfrac{5}{4}OPT.
\end{equation}
The proof of \eqref{eq:FI.upperbound} employs a charging argument.  Consider the optimal AI strategy and an arbitrary AI chain within that strategy.  Using independence of failure events, we can relate the failure probability of the AI chain to the expected number of steps within the chain that would fail for the prophet.  This lets us approximate the expected cost of an AI chain (i.e., the expected number of repetitions needed for success) by the expected number of failed steps within it, the latter of which contributes to the number of components in $\mathcal{C}$ and hence to the fragmentation index. See Appendix~\ref{app:FI} for further details.

The next step in the proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:fragmentation} is to argue that $FI$ is not much smaller than $OPT$. We begin with an analysis under the assumption that $\manualTime{i} \geq 1$ for all $s_i \in \mathcal{S}$. That is, for each step $s_i$, completing the step manually is at least as costly as managing an AI tool to complete the step in a single attempt (with guaranteed success).  Under this assumption, we can show that

\begin{equation}
\label{eq:FI.lowerbound.4}
    FI \geq \tfrac{1}{4}OPT.
\end{equation}

To give intuition for \eqref{eq:FI.lowerbound.4}, note that one way to design an AI strategy is to use the following greedy strategy: chain steps together until the failure probability of the resulting AI chain would exceed $1/2$, at which point we terminate the chain and begin anew. Each such chain requires, in expectation, at most $2$ executions to complete successfully. The steps in each such chain also contribute a constant amount to the fragmentation index, as there is a constant probability that at least one step in the chain or immediately following the chain would fail. Since the optimal AI strategy is only better than this greedy strategy, we conclude (after taking care to avoid double-counting errors on the boundary between two chains) that the fragmentation index cannot be much less than OPT.

We also provide a bound on how much further this analysis could be improved: Example~\ref{ex:FI.approx.not.2} in Appendix~\ref{app:FI} describes a problem instance where $FI$ is strictly less than half of $OPT$, even under our assumption that $\manualTime{i} \geq 1$ for all $s_i \in \mathcal{S}$.

\section{Discussion: Worker Specialization and Non-Linearity of AI Benefits}

\subsection{Impact of AI Deployment on Worker Skill and Specialization}
\label{sec:discussion.skills}

While the fragmentation index captures the short-run impact of AI deployment on step execution time, in the long run the firm can also adjust worker skill requirements and workforce specialization alongside its AI strategy to lower costs. 
That is, the long-run wage bill combines an (expected) time requirement with a worker skill requirement, where skill reflects manual execution or AI management. 
Long-run gains from AI therefore need not necessarily come from time savings. 
The wage bill can fall through lower completion time, lower required skill, or both. 
These mechanisms are not mutually exclusive, and neither is necessary on its own.
In particular, AI may increase effective completion time for a subset of tasks while reducing the skill required to perform them, and still lower the wage bill. 
% For example, a firm may replace manual execution by a high-skill worker with AI management by a lower-skill worker, even if repeated attempts and monitoring lengthen expected completion time, because the reduction in required skill and wage rate dominates.%\footnote{
% In the geometric representation discussed in Appendix~\ref{app:model_handoff_discussion}, the wage bill associated with a task is the area of a rectangle with width equal to the time requirement and height equal to the skill requirement. 
% The area can fall when one dimension declines even if the other rises.
% } 

More generally, sectors and occupations may lie in different regions of this time--skill tradeoff. 
When AI mainly reduces time requirements while holding skill requirements fixed or raising them, our framework predicts worker upskilling. 
When AI mainly reduces skill requirements with limited time savings, or even higher completion time, our framework predicts worker deskilling. 
Importantly, both forces can operate simultaneously and the direction of net skill change depends on which effect dominates in equilibrium. 
Appendix~\ref{app:discussion_examples_reskilling} gives three numerical examples showing that productivity gains from AI in the long run do not necessarily come in the form of reduced time savings, and that our framework supports both upskilling and deskilling of workers.





\subsection{Non-Linear Impacts of AI Improvements}
\label{sec:nonlinear_impact}

The full economic impact of AI can take substantial time and investment to materialize, because its benefits may not be realized until large-scale reorganization of production becomes feasible. 
This implies that the marginal value of improvements in AI quality need not be monotone, and that adoption of AI may follow a J-curve pattern \citep{brynjolfsson2021productivity}. 
In our model, this pattern arises endogenously because the firm optimizes jointly over AI deployment and job design strategies. 
One can think of parameter $\alpha \in (0,1]$ in our model as a metric of the quality of a general-purpose AI technology, taken to be the probability that a task of normalized difficulty $1$ is completed successfully.  
We can then model general technology improvements as increasing the value of $\alpha$.  
For a given \emph{fixed} AI strategy $\T$ and job design $\J$, the total cost can be expressed as a polynomial in $(1/\alpha)$.  
As a result, the cost of $\T$ and $\J$ shifts in a continuous and smooth manner as $\alpha$ increases, with monotone marginal gains to technology improvements.  
However, the optimal choice of design involves taking the minimum-cost solution over many possible AI strategies and job designs in \eqref{eq:totalcost_with_handoff}.  
AI strategies with a higher degree of AI chaining will naturally involve higher powers of $1/\alpha$ in their cost expressions, meaning that they are more sensitive to changes in $\alpha$ and become preferable only at higher values of $\alpha$ (i.e., as AI becomes more effective as a tool). 
This naturally leads to scenarios where the productivity improvements of AI integration are modest at low levels of quality but can grow sharply past a certain inflection point.

Figure~\ref{fig:Jcurve_example} shows an example production chain consisting of two steps in which, for modest values of $\alpha$, the firm optimally does not deploy AI (left graph), so marginal improvements in AI quality have zero value (right graph). 
Once AI quality reaches the threshold at which the minimum-cost way of production requires workflow reorganization and assigning a step to AI, the marginal return to further quality improvements jumps discontinuously. 
As AI quality rises further, additional organizational adjustments make longer AI chains optimal at higher values of $\alpha$, which makes the effect even more pronounced. 
Appendix~\ref{app:discussion_examples_nonlinear_impact} reports the cost parameters for this example and details how the firm's optimal choices are computed.
